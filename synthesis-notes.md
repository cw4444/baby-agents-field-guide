# Field Notes on Agency, Selves, and Cognition

I found a long Michael Levin essay in this folder and pulled out the core idea: these terms are not treated as rigid binaries, but as observer-dependent lenses that help scientists and engineers predict, control, and discover.

## The central frame

The essay argues that words like `memory`, `agency`, `decision-making`, `intelligence`, `life`, and `consciousness` work best when they are used operationally.

That means asking:

- What is the term for?
- How do we recognize it?
- How do we measure it?
- What scale of analysis is it defined at?

The key move is to avoid pretending there is one “correct” definition that applies at every level, from molecules to organisms to minds.

## The recurring pattern

Several concepts are presented as continua rather than all-or-nothing categories:

- `Agency` is framed as a perceptual control loop that tries to minimize error relative to expectations.
- `Goal-directedness` can exist in minimal forms, not just in conscious beings.
- `Intelligence` is the capacity to navigate a problem space competently despite novelty and barriers.
- `Life` is treated as a cognitively organized, metabolically constrained, physically embodied process that scales up component competencies.
- `Consciousness` is linked to the continuous interpretation of memory and the maintenance of a coherent self-story.

## The most memorable metaphor

The essay repeatedly uses the idea of a `cognitive light cone`.

That phrase is doing a lot of work:

- It marks the spatiotemporal range that matters for understanding a system.
- It explains why some phenomena look mechanical at one scale and agentic at another.
- It gives a way to talk about how much of the past, future, and surrounding context must be considered to model a system well.

In this framing, a complex agent is not just local physics. It is a system whose meaningful behavior extends across time, memory, prediction, and environment.

## A compact reading of the definitions

- `Agent`: a system with a control loop, expectations, and action.
- `Self`: an emergent whole with goals, preferences, and memory that are not reducible to any one part.
- `Goal`: a useful description whenever goal-based control gives traction for prediction and intervention.
- `Intelligence`: competent navigation of a problem space.
- `Embodiment`: not only a body in 3D space, but any space in which perception, prediction, and action can occur.
- `Life`: a persistent embodied cognitive system under metabolic constraints.
- `Consciousness`: the lived process of interpreting memory and sustaining a coherent internal story.

## Why it matters

The essay’s practical value is that it resists false binaries.

Instead of asking whether a system “has” agency, life, or consciousness in some absolute sense, it asks how much a given lens helps us:

- understand the system,
- intervene in it,
- predict its behavior,
- and relate to it ethically.

That makes the whole framework feel less like a dictionary and more like a research instrument.

## One-line takeaway

The core thesis is that minds, lives, and agents are best understood as context-sensitive, multi-scale control systems rather than as sharply bounded labels.
